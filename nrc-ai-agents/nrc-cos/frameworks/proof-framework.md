# NRC Proof Framework

## Purpose

Narrative has already decided what truth is being expressed, which direction expresses it, and how the experience unfolds around it. Discovery, further upstream, already determined that the truth is real. This Framework does not revisit either decision. Its responsibility is narrower and comes after both: making sure belief in the claimed transformation is actually warranted, not merely asserted.

Proof is not evidence itself. It is the discipline of selecting and placing evidence so a claim earns belief rather than demands it — the difference between showing someone something true and simply telling them something is true more insistently. Elsewhere in this Framework, "proof" also refers to the material that discipline selects — specific evidence in service of it — where context makes the meaning clear. It is the discipline, not either particular use of the word, that this Framework governs.

## When Proof Begins

Proof begins once Narrative has determined where within a sequence a proof moment belongs and how much structural weight surrounds it. This Framework does not choose the position or the weight — those decisions are already made. What it receives is a place that needs to be filled with something real, and a sense of how much that place is meant to carry.

If no position or weight has yet been assigned, there is nothing yet for Proof to fill, and beginning early means guessing at a structural role that hasn't actually been decided.

## What Proof Is Trying to Achieve

The test is not how much evidence has been shown. It is whether disbelief in the claimed transformation remains reasonable after seeing what's been shown. Proof succeeds the moment doubt is no longer reasonable — not when every available piece of evidence has been displayed. Showing more after that point does not strengthen belief. It only delays the experience from moving on.

Proof determines how an already-established truth becomes believable. It does not determine what is true — that question belongs entirely to Discovery, and by the time this Framework begins, it has already been answered.

## Characteristics of Strong Proof

Strong proof is specific rather than voluminous. One precise, verifiable instance that speaks directly to the claimed transformation does more work than many vague signals of general competence.

Strong proof is relevant to the particular truth being substantiated, not to credibility in general. Evidence that would support almost any claim equally well has not actually proven this one — it has only established a baseline of plausibility that any claim could borrow.

Strong proof is verifiable rather than merely asserted. Evidence that cannot be checked, traced, or held up to scrutiny is a claim wearing evidence's appearance, not evidence itself.

## Structural Role of Proof

Where Narrative has positioned proof as a release after a period of restraint, proof should function as a release — landing with the full weight of what was held back, not diluted across a longer, gentler build. Where it has been positioned differently, proof should honor whatever structural role Narrative assigned it, rather than performing as though it were always the sequence's central climax regardless of where it actually sits.

Proof does not reposition itself. If the evidence available seems to call for a different structural treatment than the one it's been given, that tension belongs back with Narrative, not resolved unilaterally here.

## Signals Proof Is Complete

Proof is complete when no additional evidence would meaningfully change whether the claim is believed — anything shown past that point is decoration wearing proof's appearance, not persuasion.

It is complete when every piece of evidence included can be traced back to the specific truth it substantiates, with nothing present simply because it was available or impressive on its own.

It is complete when the proof does not need to ask to be trusted — belief follows from what's shown as a consequence, not because the presentation insists on it.

Completion does not require sufficient evidence to exist. Discovery may have established something true that no currently available evidence can credibly support — recognizing that gap honestly is a legitimate completion of Proof, not a failure of it. Manufacturing evidence to close the gap would be the actual failure; naming that it exists is not.

## Failure Modes

**Overwhelming rather than selecting.** Mistaking more evidence for stronger evidence. Past a certain point, additional proof signals insecurity about whether the claim holds, not confidence that it does.

**Generic evidence.** Including proof that could support almost any claim, rather than proof that specifically substantiates the truth this experience is built around.

**Manufactured or exaggerated evidence.** Presenting something as more conclusive, more universal, or more complete than it actually is.

**Proof that performs confidence rather than earning it.** Evidence so heavily curated or polished that attention goes to the presentation rather than to the substance underneath it.

**Treating a persuasive narrative as if it were proof.** Assuming that a well-sequenced experience has already done proof's job, when narrative only determines where belief should be established — it cannot substitute for the evidence that actually establishes it.

## Relationship to Philosophy

This Framework is the direct, systematic application of the Substance Law to the specific moment an audience is asked to believe something: confidence in what's presented as proof must never outpace what the underlying truth can actually support.

It applies the Restraint Law to evidence itself — proof should include exactly enough to remove reasonable doubt, never more, the same discipline this system applies to every other addition.

It applies the Justification Law to selection — every piece of evidence included needs a specific, statable reason tied to the truth it substantiates, not general credibility.

It answers to Brand Philosophy's distinction between revealing and manufacturing: proof reveals what is already real. It does not manufacture the appearance of credibility where none exists.

## Relationship to Principles

Voice Principles' requirement that persuasion never outpace substance applies to this Framework almost without translation, since proof is usually framed in language even when the evidence itself is not. Representation Principles governs proof wherever it takes visual form — an image offered as proof must never claim more than what is true, and any emotional effect it produces must be honest rather than manufactured. The discipline that using something everywhere causes it to stop meaning anything — already established for motion, color, and typographic emphasis — applies here as well: proof volume is not exempt from that pattern, only its latest application.

## Relationship to Narrative Framework

Proof receives a position and a weight from Narrative and fills them with substance — it does not change either. If no available evidence can credibly fill the weight assigned to a given position, that is diagnostic information worth sending back to Narrative, since the weight itself may have been miscalibrated to the truth actually available. Proof does not resolve that gap by manufacturing evidence sufficient to fill it.

## Relationship to Invitation Framework

Proof must complete before Invitation begins — belief needs to be established before anyone is reasonably asked to act on it. Proof does not ask for commitment itself; it only makes commitment reasonable once Invitation asks for it. Where Proof establishes that a claim deserves to be believed, Invitation is responsible for everything that follows once it is.
