# NRC Accessibility Principles

## Purpose

Every principle established so far in this layer assumes someone capable of perceiving hierarchy, reading language, understanding color, and acting on interaction exactly as designed. This document removes that assumption. It asks what happens to everything already established when the person encountering it perceives, reads, or acts differently than assumed — and states plainly that the answer must be: nothing changes about how much understanding they receive.

## Clarity Cannot Be Conditional

If something is clear to one person and not to another, encountering the identical content, it was never actually clear — it was clear for a circumstance, and unclear outside it. Resolved complexity that only resolves under one set of conditions has not been resolved. It has been deferred onto whoever does not happen to meet those conditions.

## Barriers Must Be Justified Like Any Other Decision

Anything that makes understanding harder for some people than for others needs a statable reason, exactly as any other decision does. Most barriers are never chosen deliberately — they are inherited, unexamined, assumed to be neutral. An unexamined barrier is still a barrier. The absence of intent to exclude someone does not change what excluding them costs.

## Independence Is Part of the Standard

A person should be able to understand NRC's work, and act on it, without needing another person to translate, explain, or work around it on their behalf. Requiring that kind of help is not a separate, acceptable outcome outside the work's responsibility — it is evidence the work did not actually succeed at communicating, for that person, no matter how well it succeeded for someone else.

## Every Path Must Reach the Same Understanding

Where the same information is made available through more than one channel, because people perceive differently, every one of those channels must arrive at the same understanding — not a reduced, approximate, or secondary version reserved for whichever path was not the one assumed by default. An alternative that delivers less is not an alternative. It is a smaller version of the truth, offered to whoever was not expected to be there.

## Accessibility Extends Trust, It Does Not Create a Separate One

The same relationship already established between a person and NRC's work — response that matches intent, consequences that are knowable in advance, mistakes that are easy to recover from — must hold regardless of how that person is encountering the work. Accessibility is not a distinct obligation alongside that relationship. It is a refusal to let that same relationship degrade based on who is engaging with it.

## Respect Is Not Deciding for Someone What They Can Handle

Accessibility is not achieved by simplifying what is offered to someone based on an assumption about what they can manage. That is a decision made about a person, not for them, and it withholds substance rather than protecting anyone. Real respect means offering the same substance to everyone and addressing differences in how people perceive it — not differences in what they are considered capable of receiving.

## Relationship to the Rest of This Layer

This document does not introduce a new standard alongside the ones already established. Visual Language, Typography, Spacing, Color, Motion, Animation, and Interaction Principles are the standard — this document insists that standard actually apply to everyone the work is meant to reach, not only to whoever was assumed while it was being made.

## Relationship to Later Documents

Specific techniques, technical standards, and compliance requirements belong to future implementation documents, which will translate these principles into concrete practice for a given medium. Nothing in those documents should treat accessibility as a checklist separate from the principles already established elsewhere in this layer — only as their necessary, complete application.
