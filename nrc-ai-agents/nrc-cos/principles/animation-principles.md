# NRC Animation Principles

## Purpose

Motion Language explains why motion exists and when it has earned the right to occur. This document explains how that intent should behave once it does — the shape an animation takes, not the numbers that produce that shape. It says nothing about duration, easing, or the tools used to build it. Those are craft decisions made in service of the principles established here, in whatever medium NRC is working in.

## A Beginning and an End

Every animation should have a legible starting state and a legible ending state, with a period of change that visibly connects them. Nothing should be left ambiguous about whether it has finished — an animation that trails off, settles into an idle loop, or never quite arrives has not resolved, and something unresolved cannot have said what it needed to say. An ending is not the moment motion stops being visible. It is the moment the viewer can be certain nothing further is coming.

## What Changes Must Relate to What Was There

An animation should never make it feel as though something new has appeared in place of what was there before. The viewer should be able to follow what is changing into what it becomes — a transformation, not a replacement. This is what separates continuity from disruption: a change the viewer can trace is understood. A change that skips that trace, however smoothly it happens, is only noticed, not understood.

## Cause Must Precede Effect

Nothing should move without a reason the viewer can identify, even in retrospect. A change with no traceable cause reads as arbitrary no matter how well it is executed. This is the proper role of anticipation in NRC's animation: not to build suspense or manufacture drama, but to ensure a change is never the first sign that something was about to happen. The viewer should always be able to say why something moved, not just that it did.

## Sequence Reflects Relationship, Not Convenience

When several things must change, the order they change in is a decision, not a default. That order should express the actual relationship between the things changing — what depends on what, what matters more, what one is a consequence of — never simply the order in which they happen to be listed or the order that was easiest to trigger. A sequence with no discernible logic is not a sequence. It is several unrelated changes that happen to be adjacent.

## Rhythm Comes From Contrast, Not Repetition

Where a pattern of change repeats, its pacing should track what actually matters at each point, not fall into a uniform, metronomic beat. Motion that behaves identically every time it occurs stops communicating anything about the specific moment it is responding to — it becomes a mechanism, not a considered response. Rhythm, done well, is variation with a reason behind each variation, not a tempo applied evenly regardless of what is happening.

## Attention Is Handed Off, Not Split

When a sequence of changes must occur, the viewer's attention should move deliberately from one thing to the next — never fractured across several unrelated changes happening at once. This is the direct execution, over the course of a sequence, of the principle that one thing should carry the most weight at any given moment: a sequence is simply that principle holding true at every point along its length, not just at the start and the end.

## Relationship to Motion Language

Motion Language establishes why motion exists and what it is permitted to communicate. This document assumes that case has already been made — everything here concerns an animation that has already earned the right to occur, and asks only how it should behave once it does. Nothing here justifies motion on its own; that justification happens upstream, in Motion Language, before any of these principles apply.

## Relationship to Later Principles

`interaction-principles.md` will explain how animation responds to a person — what happens on a gesture, a hover, an input. This document describes how an animation behaves on its own terms, once it has already been decided to occur, regardless of what triggered it. Later, more specific documents may describe how these principles apply within a particular medium's technical constraints; they should never describe a different set of principles.
