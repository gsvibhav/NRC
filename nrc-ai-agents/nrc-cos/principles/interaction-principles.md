# NRC Interaction Principles

## Purpose

Animation Principles explains how a system's own motion should behave. This document explains how the system should behave in relation to a person — what happens between someone forming an intent and the system responding to it. It is not about buttons, gestures, or platforms. It is about what makes that relationship trustworthy, regardless of what device or medium carries it.

## Response Must Match What Was Asked

An interaction's response should be proportionate to the action that caused it — not larger, not smaller, and never disconnected from what was actually done. A small action deserves a response that feels like its natural continuation, not an event of its own. Nothing about a system's response should feel like it came from somewhere other than the person's own intent.

## Consequences Should Be Knowable Before They're Chosen

Before someone commits to something significant, what will happen should already be knowable — not necessarily explained in full, but never hidden. This matters most where a consequence cannot be undone: that fact belongs to the moment before the choice is made, not to the moment after. A person should never discover irreversibility as a surprise.

## The Weight of Commitment Should Match the Weight of the Consequence

Small, reversible actions deserve to be effortless. Significant or irreversible ones deserve to require real, deliberate commitment. Treating the two the same is a failure in either direction — unnecessary friction on something trivial insults a person's judgment, and too little friction on something serious treats them carelessly.

## Trust Is Built by Being the Same Every Time

A system should behave identically for the same kind of action, every time it occurs. Interaction that changes its own rules in order to be clever or unexpected has decided novelty matters more than being relied upon. Confidence is what a person has after learning, correctly and repeatedly, what to expect — never what a system produces by surprising them into feeling something.

## Recovery Should Never Be Harder Than the Mistake

A mistake is ordinary, not a failure requiring penance. The way back from an unintended action should be at least as available as the action that caused it — never buried, never requiring more effort to undo than it took to do. A system that makes recovery difficult is quietly punishing a person for trusting it enough to act.

## Interaction Is Never Requested for Its Own Sake

A system should never ask for interaction merely to prove it can respond, to hold attention, or to manufacture engagement that wasn't already there. It exists to respond to intent a person already has — not to generate intent that wouldn't otherwise exist, in order to justify its own presence.

## Relationship to Animation Principles

Animation Principles governs how the system moves on its own terms, once motion has already been decided to occur. This document governs what happens when that motion — or any system response — is set in motion by a person rather than occurring independently. The two should never contradict each other: an interaction that behaves well by these principles but animates in a way that violates Animation Principles has not actually behaved well.

## Relationship to Later Principles

`accessibility.md` will extend several of these principles — proportionate response, knowable consequences, forgiving recovery — into requirements for people encountering NRC's work under different conditions and constraints. Nothing in that document should describe a different relationship between a person and the system than the one established here; it should describe the same relationship, held to account for everyone.
