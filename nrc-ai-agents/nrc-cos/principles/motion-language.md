# NRC Motion Language

## Purpose

Visual Language explains how NRC organizes what is seen. This document explains how NRC treats what changes over time. It does not define how motion moves — no easing, no duration, no technique. It explains why motion should exist at all, when it has earned the right to, and what it is allowed to communicate. `animation-principles.md` will explain execution. This document explains intent.

## Motion Is a Statement, Not Decoration

Motion must satisfy the same test as any other creative decision: a statable reason for existing. It exists to communicate something stillness cannot — a change in state, a relationship between two moments, a sequence whose order matters. If the same understanding would arrive equally well without it, the motion was not necessary. It was decoration, borrowing the appearance of communication without earning it.

## Motion Is Hierarchy Across Time

One thing should carry the most weight at a time. Motion is what happens when that weight needs to move — not a spectacle competing for attention, but attention being redirected on purpose. At any moment, one thing should be changing, not several. Several things shifting at once is the temporal version of giving everything equal visual weight: it does not direct attention, it disperses it.

## Stillness Is the Default

Stillness does not need to justify itself. Motion does. A moment of stillness is not a gap waiting to be filled — it is often the correct, complete answer, and deserves the same deliberateness as any other choice, not the treatment of an unfinished page waiting for someone to add movement to it.

## Motion That Never Stops Has Stopped Meaning Anything

Motion means something because it is a departure from stillness. Anything that continues indefinitely without resolving — a loop, an ambient animation, a repeating flourish — eventually stops being noticed at all. It becomes background, not communication. Motion that is always happening has nothing left to say, because nothing about it is a decision anymore; it has simply become the page's resting state, and the actual resting state, stillness, is left with no way to signal that nothing here demands attention right now.

## Trust Through Consistency, Not Urgency

Motion is one of the easiest tools available for manufacturing a feeling the content has not earned — urgency, excitement, importance that isn't actually there. NRC's motion should never be used this way. It should behave predictably, arrive at what it promises, and never rush someone toward a decision faster than the content itself would justify. Trust in motion is built the same way trust is built anywhere else in NRC's work: through consistency, not persuasion.

## Confidence Arrives Once

Motion that repeats itself, loops for emphasis, or plays again to make sure a point landed is behaving like something that does not trust itself to have been understood the first time. Confident motion happens once, resolves, and stops. It does not ask to be watched twice.

## Relationship to Visual Language

Visual Language defines how hierarchy, clarity, and restraint are organized in space. This document applies the same principles to time — motion is not a separate discipline with its own rules, it is what those principles look like the moment something needs to change rather than simply be arranged. Anything written here that contradicts Visual Language is wrong, not the other way around.

## Relationship to Later Principles

`animation-principles.md` will translate this intent into craft — pacing, the character of arrivals and departures, how much motion is spent and where. `interaction-principles.md` will translate it into how motion responds to a person, rather than simply occurring on its own. Neither should establish a new reason for motion to exist beyond what is written here; they exist to execute this intent well, not to decide when motion is warranted in the first place.
