# NRC Performance Standards

## Purpose

Every principle established elsewhere in this layer protects a person's attention from being spent without receiving value in return — restraint against unnecessary addition, motion that never overstays its reason, interaction that never asks for more than it needs. This document applies that same protection to time. A delay, an interruption, or unnecessary effort asked of a system is not a technical inconvenience. It is attention taken and nothing given back for it.

## Every Wait Must Be Justified Like Any Other Decision

A delay is not neutral simply because it is common, or because building something instant is harder than building something slow. The same question applies here as anywhere else: is there a reason this person should have to wait? If the answer is no, the wait is a cost being extracted without anyone having agreed to pay it.

## Unnecessary Work Is Still an Addition

Doing more than a task requires — more processing, more complexity, more moved than the outcome needed — is the same failure as any other unjustified addition, only paid for in someone's time instead of their attention to visual clutter. A capability being available to do more is not a reason to do it, here any more than anywhere else.

## Response Speed Is a Form of Weight

How quickly something responds signals how much significance was assigned to the request that caused it. A simple action deserves an immediate response; treating it as anything less immediate misjudges its scale, in exactly the way giving an unimportant element outsized visual weight would misjudge its importance. This is the same principle Motion Language calls hierarchy across time, applied to a dimension motion alone does not reach: not whether something visibly changes, but how long a person waits before anything happens at all. It is also the timing counterpart to Interaction Principles' requirement that a response match what was asked — that principle governs what comes back; this one governs how quickly.

## Consistency Builds the Same Trust Here As Everywhere Else

A system that responds quickly sometimes and slowly other times, for the same kind of request, breaks the same predictability already required of motion, interaction, typography, and color. Reliability in time is not a separate concern requiring its own justification. It is the same principle already established elsewhere, simply measured in a different unit.

## Effort Must Never Be Performed for Its Own Sake

A system should never be made to appear slower, busier, or more effortful than necessary in order to seem more thorough, more premium, or more considered. That manufactures an impression the underlying work has not earned — the same failure already prohibited for motion and color, expressed here through time instead of movement or hue.

## Interruption Costs More Than Delay

Being made to wait is a cost. Being made to wait after already receiving something, or having something change unexpectedly partway through, is a greater one — it breaks a continuity the person had already begun to rely on and asks them to re-orient a second time. Protecting attention means protecting continuity as much as it means protecting speed.

## Relationship to the Rest of This Layer

Nothing in this document introduces a new value. Restraint, justification, hierarchy, and consistency are already established elsewhere in this layer; this document is simply where those same principles are held to account against time, rather than against space, language, or color.

## Relationship to Later Documents

Specific engineering targets, tooling, and technical practice belong to future Frameworks and implementation documents, which will translate these principles into practice for a given technology and platform. Nothing in those documents should treat performance as an engineering discipline separate from the respect for attention already established here — only as its practical execution.
