# NRC Typography Principles

## Purpose

Visual Language explains how information is organized in space. This document explains how language specifically should be presented within that organization, so that meaning stays clear and the words themselves remain trustworthy. It is not about which typeface communicates that, or at what size — it is about what typography is required to do, regardless of the letterforms used to do it.

## Hierarchy Through Restraint

Typographic hierarchy should be built from the smallest number of distinctions that make the intended reading order clear — not from applying every available means of emphasis at once. A page where several things compete for the most attention has not established a hierarchy; it has simply made several claims to importance and left the reader to adjudicate between them. One level of emphasis should be unmistakably the most important. Everything else should visibly recede.

## Emphasis Must Be Earned

Every instance of emphasis needs a reason distinct from the general sense that something seemed important. Emphasis applied often stops functioning as emphasis — if everything is highlighted, nothing has actually been distinguished. Emphasis also must never make a claim look more certain or more significant than the claim itself can support; typographic weight is not a substitute for the substance underneath it, and using it that way is a visual version of the same failure as making a claim the work cannot back up.

## Reading Effort Should Match the Idea, Not Exceed It

The difficulty of reading something should come from the idea being communicated, never from the way it has been presented. A simple idea does not deserve to require extra work to parse. A genuinely complex idea's difficulty belongs to the idea — typography's job is to make sure none of that difficulty is being added to, not to contribute difficulty of its own on top of it.

## Consistency Is What Makes Reading Fast

The same kind of information should look the same way every time it appears. A reader who has learned how one kind of statement is presented should never have to relearn how to read it the next time it shows up. Inconsistency forces re-interpretation at every encounter, which is attention spent on parsing rather than on understanding — the opposite of what typography exists to do.

## Confidence Is Precision, Not Volume

Typography that shouts — through scale, through excess, through insistence — signals doubt in the words themselves, not confidence in them. Language that is actually strong does not need visual amplification to be believed. Precision, not volume, is what typographic confidence looks like: exactly the emphasis required, and nothing offered beyond it to compensate for uncertainty about whether the words alone would be enough.

## Plain Language Deserves Room

Text surrounded by space, rather than crowded by competing information, is text that has been trusted to stand on its own. Giving language room to be read without contest is itself a form of respect for a reader's attention — it says the words were considered sufficient, not that they needed reinforcement to land.

## Relationship to Interaction Principles

Interaction Principles establishes that a system should behave the same way for the same kind of action, every time. Typography carries that same obligation into language: the same kind of statement should read the same way, every time it appears, so a reader can extend to written communication the same trust they extend to any other consistent part of NRC's work.

## Relationship to Later Principles

`color-principles.md` and `spacing-principles.md` will each govern a different dimension of how this same language is presented — color will govern meaning carried through hue and contrast, spacing will govern the measured relationships around and within text. Neither should establish a theory of hierarchy, emphasis, or restraint that differs from what is written here; both exist to carry it into their own domain.
