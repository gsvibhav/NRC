# NRC Voice Principles

## Purpose

Design Language describes the character NRC's work should have. Typography Principles describes how already-written language becomes visible. This document sits between them: it governs how language is created in the first place — the sentences themselves, before anyone has decided how to typeset them. It is not about persuasion technique, marketing craft, or platform-specific copywriting. It is about what makes written language trustworthy, regardless of the medium it eventually appears in.

## Every Sentence Needs a Reason to Exist

The same test that governs any other decision governs a sentence: does it change what the reader understands? A sentence that only restates, decorates, or performs effort does not belong, no matter how well it is written. Restraint in writing is not a matter of using fewer words for their own sake — it is deleting anything that isn't actually doing work.

## Confidence Is Precision, Not Intensifiers

Writing that reaches for superlatives and amplifying language — extraordinary, revolutionary, incredible — is compensating for doubt about whether the underlying claim is strong enough to stand on its own. Precise language that says exactly what is true does not need amplification to be believed. Intensifiers are to writing what visual shouting is to typography: evidence the words alone were not trusted.

## Persuasion Must Never Outpace Substance

Writing may argue for something. It must never claim more certainty, more importance, or more inevitability than the underlying substance can support. The moment language persuades beyond what is actually true, it has stopped being honest — regardless of how well it works. Effective and honest are not the same test, and honest is the one that governs here.

## Clarity Is Resolved Before the Sentence Is Written

A sentence that is hard to read because the thinking behind it was never fully worked out is not complex writing. It is unresolved thinking, presented as prose. The difficulty a reader experiences should belong entirely to the idea being communicated — never to writing that is standing in for thought that hasn't finished happening yet.

## Writing Should Say a Thing Once

Restating a point in different words, in case the first version didn't land, is not reinforcement. It is evidence the writer does not trust what was already said. Committing to one clear statement of something, and moving on, is what confident writing looks like. Saying it again is not more persuasive. It is simply longer.

## Rhythm Comes From Variation, Not Uniformity

Writing that varies its pace — a short sentence after several long ones, a pause where the idea calls for one — has rhythm. Writing that is mechanically uniform in length and structure regardless of what it is saying has cadence, not rhythm; it has stopped responding to the content and started repeating a pattern for its own sake. Pacing should track what is actually being said, the same way any other rhythm in NRC's work does.

## Narrative Order Reflects Relationship, Not Convenience

When writing must cover several ideas in sequence, their order should reflect how they actually relate to each other — cause before effect, foundation before conclusion — not the order they occurred to the writer or happened to be easiest to explain. An order with no discernible logic is not a narrative. It is a list of things that happen to be adjacent.

## Relationship to Design Language

Design Language describes NRC's character in the abstract — unhurried, precise, recognizable through restraint. This document is what that character requires of actual sentences. Where Design Language says what the work should feel like, this document says what has to be true of how it is written for that feeling to survive being put into words.

## Relationship to Typography Principles

Typography Principles governs how language, once written, becomes visible — hierarchy, emphasis, legibility. This document governs the language before that happens. The two should never conflict: writing that satisfies this document but is then typeset in a way that violates Typography Principles has not actually been well served, and typesetting that satisfies Typography Principles applied to writing that violates this document has nothing worth presenting well.

## Relationship to Later Frameworks

Storytelling and narrative frameworks will apply these principles at the scale of a full experience — a page, a presentation, a sequence of sections. Nothing at that scale should establish a different standard for language than the one established here; a framework's narrative arc is simply these same sentence-level principles, sustained over a longer form.
