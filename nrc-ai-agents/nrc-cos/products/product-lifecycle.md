# NRC Product Lifecycle

## Purpose

The Product Layer Architecture defines what a Product is — its ownership, its boundaries, what makes it complete — the same way the Design Constitution defines what a creative decision must satisfy. Neither document says anything about time. A Product does not simply exist; it comes into being, develops, becomes ready for review, is sometimes revised, is eventually frozen, and one day may be retired. This document governs that movement.

Product Layer Architecture is a Product's constitution. Product Lifecycle is the process that constitution operates under — the same relationship the repository's general governance has to the Design Constitution itself, applied one layer down, specific to how a Product's own timeline unfolds. This document does not redefine what a Product is, and it does not redefine repository governance. It only defines how a Product moves through the stages every Product eventually passes through.

## Product Creation

A new Product should exist when a real situation requires its own run through the Framework chain — its own Discovery, however quickly completed, because the truth that chain would find is genuinely different from any truth already established.

Extending an existing Product is different. It applies when the same underlying discovered truth still holds, and what's changing is something further down the chain — new evidence available for the same claimed transformation, a different moment for the same invitation — without the truth itself needing to be found again.

The test is Discovery's own test, applied at the boundary between Products rather than within one: if running Discovery again on this situation would produce a different truth than the one an existing Product already established, this is a new Product. If it would produce the same truth, this is a continuation of the one that already exists.

## Product Development

A Product develops by running the Framework chain in order — Discovery, then Concept Exploration, then Narrative, then Proof, then Invitation — each stage reaching its own completion before the next begins, exactly as each Framework already requires of itself.

Development is not required to be linear in the sense of never looking back. Where a later stage reveals that an earlier one needs reconsidering — Concept Exploration finding no concept can express what Discovery found, Narrative finding a concept doesn't hold together across a sequence — sending that finding back upstream is normal development, not a failure of it. What development is not permitted to do is skip a stage rather than revisit it, or treat a stage's output as settled before that stage has actually reached its own completion.

Development derives entirely from Philosophy, Principles, Frameworks, and the Product Layer Architecture already governing what a Product must be. It introduces nothing beyond applying what already exists to one real situation.

## Product Review

A Product becomes ready for architectural review the moment it satisfies the Product Layer Architecture's own completion standard — every stage of the Framework chain has reached its own signals of completion, or has honestly recorded the legitimate absence that Proof and Invitation both permit, rather than manufacturing what wasn't there.

A Product is not ready before that point, regardless of how much work has gone into it — an incomplete chain has nothing yet for review to evaluate as a whole. A Product should not sit completed without moving to review either; once the standard is met, review is the next step, not a stage that can be deferred indefinitely while treating the Product as though it were already settled.

## Product Revision

Refinement and architectural change are different things, and only one of them belongs at the Product level.

Refinement adjusts a Product's own specific decisions without changing what they derive from — updating evidence as circumstances change, resequencing within an arc that remains structurally the same, resizing an invitation as trust changes. Refinement stays entirely within decisions the Product already owns.

Architectural change is different: it's what's actually being asked for when a revision keeps requiring the same kind of adjustment, or when honoring a Principle or a Framework's discipline no longer seems to produce the right outcome for the situation. That is not a Product-level problem to keep patching. It's a signal that something upstream may need to change, and it belongs in the governance that already exists for revising a Principle or a Framework — never resolved by repeatedly revising the Product around it.

This distinction holds at any point in a Product's lifecycle — during the ordinary review-and-revise cycle before freeze, and equally after a Product has already been frozen. What changes between the two is not what revision means, only the governance around when it happens: before freeze, revision refines work still in development; after freeze, revision becomes its own recorded, traceable change, per Product Versioning.

## Product Freeze

Freezing a Product makes what Product Layer Architecture's "What Products Own" already establishes as belonging to it immutable as a historical record. None of it can be silently altered once frozen — changing any part of it is a revision, subject to the same distinction Product Revision already draws.

What may still change outside NRC-COS is how those frozen decisions are technically realized. The Product Layer Architecture already draws this line: a Product stops before implementation, and implementation belongs to whatever production system follows it. That production system can iterate freely, on its own timeline, in whatever technology it uses — as long as what it produces stays faithful to what the frozen Product actually decided. Freezing the decisions does not freeze their realization.

## Product Versioning

Versioning, at this layer, is a principle rather than a scheme — no numbering system belongs here, only the requirement that change be traceable.

When a Product is legitimately revised, what changed and why must remain visible, not overwritten. The Product's prior state does not disappear when a new one replaces it; it becomes part of a lineage that can still be examined, the same way this system never edits a decision record after acceptance, only supersedes it with a new one that references what it replaced. A Product evolves by accumulating a visible history of change, never by quietly rewriting what it used to say.

This is how a Product evolves without touching the frozen constitutional layers above it: the layers stay exactly as they are, and everything that changes about a Product changes within the record this document already governs.

## Pattern Extraction

Patterns are never designed. They are recognized.

Nothing about a Product's own lifecycle includes a step for producing a Pattern. A Pattern becomes visible only after the fact — when a specific decision inside one frozen Product is later found, independently, inside a second, genuinely different frozen Product, without either having been built to match the other. Recognizing that repetition, and confirming it is genuine rather than coincidental, is what triggers the governance ADR-005 already establishes. Nothing in a Product's own development, review, or freeze should anticipate or reach for this outcome — a Product that is shaped in order to become a Pattern has stopped being an honest answer to its own real situation, and Patterns extracted that way are exactly the speculative kind this system already prohibits.

## Product Retirement

A Product should be retired when the truth it was built on no longer holds for the situation it addressed, when a new Product has been created that addresses the same situation more truthfully, or when the situation itself no longer exists to be addressed.

Retirement is the decision that a Product's active life has ended. Archive is where it goes once that decision is made — never deleted, moved with a record of why it was retired and, where one exists, what replaced it. Historical reference is what a retired Product continues to offer even after retirement: a discoverable record of what was once true and why, so a truth that has already run its course is never silently re-proposed by someone who never knew it had already been tried. Replacement, where it applies, is the new Product's own responsibility to reference — closing the loop the same way a superseded decision record always points to what superseded it.

## Relationship to Repository Governance

This document introduces no governance of its own. Every transition it describes — creation, the move into review, freeze, revision, retirement — that constitutes a significant decision should be recorded the same way any other significant decision in this system is recorded: as a Decision Record, in the layer already established for that purpose, append-only, never edited after acceptance, only superseded.

Product Lifecycle is not a parallel process running alongside the repository's governance. It is that same governance, already defined, applied specifically to the timeline a Product moves through.

## Future Evolution

NRC-COS may gain new Products indefinitely, each one moving through creation, development, review, freeze, revision, and eventually retirement, without any of that activity ever requiring a change to Philosophy, Principles, Frameworks, or the Product Layer Architecture itself. Products expand horizontally — more of them, addressing more real situations, each running the same chain again. The architecture they run on remains vertically stable underneath all of it.

If this document itself starts to feel like it needs frequent change to keep up with how Products are actually moving through their lifetimes, that is not a reason to revise it casually. It is a signal worth raising through the same governance it already defers to everywhere else — the same standard this document holds every Product to, held now against itself.
